The main thesis of Citrini Research’s work on AI agent impact on society — especially as articulated in their February 2026 piece “The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis” — is a stress-test scenario in which highly productive, autonomous AI agents rapidly displace human white-collar labor and erode business models built on human intermediation, triggering a negative economic feedback loop:

AI agents boost output and corporate margins while drastically reducing the need for human labor.

As workers lose well-paid jobs, aggregate consumer demand collapses, because displaced workers have less to spend.

The economy’s measured output looks strong on paper (rising productivity and GDP), but much of that output doesn’t circulate through real consumer spending — what Citrini terms “ghost GDP”.

The reduced demand then weakens companies (especially in sectors dependent on human economic activity), leading them to cut more labor and further invest in AI — a self-reinforcing loop with no natural brake.

This, in their scenario, cascades into higher unemployment, reduced consumption, mortgage and credit distress, and broader financial stress by 2028.

Citrini frames this not as a prediction but as a plausible stress test of how rapid AI displacement could structurally impair demand and destabilize economic systems.
